FORCE11 is a conference where everybody who attends is trying to improve scientific publishing in some capacity. It’s full of revolutionaries and innovators, and I finally got to attend! I literally bumped into people who created many of the scientific reform movements I’ve adored from afar: Ginny Barbour of [DORA](https://sfdora.org/), Ian Mulvany who helped create [eLife Lens](https://lens.elifesciences.org/), Daniel Katz of JOSS, and Tracy Teal of openRxiv.

**Sometimes, it’s actually pretty awesome to meet your heroes.**

## openRxiv’s Curvenote Reader launch started discussions

We were able to show many people the [Curvenote Reader product we’ve just launched with OpenRxiv](https://curvenote.com/news/openrxiv-launches-reader) and get early feedback on it.

There was a pre-conference workshop by Tracy Teal at openRxiv on the Curvenote Reader launch.

And Nokome Bentley and Tracy Teal gave a talk that wasn’t directly about Curvenote Reader, but indirectly showcased the open source technology that makes Curvenote Reader able to speak a standardized language — OXA, the [Open Exchange Architecture](https://oxa.dev/).

Nokome also gave a presentation on the FORCE11 Working Group that Rowan helped run in 2025, [Preserving Interactive Research Content](https://articles.continuousfoundation.org/articles/force11-preserving-interactive-research-content). THis working group helped inform how we work with preserving interactive and computational content in our publications and journals.

## Our Curvenote Reader & OpenRxiv project showed up in other talks organically

I was really encouraged by the reaction to our demonstrations of Curvenote Reader: **Curvenote’s Reader launch with openRxiv showed up in other talks as a _live_ reaction to our presentations about it.**

Two keynotes — Ginny Barbour of DORA and Ian Mulvany’s talk on the future of AI and publishing — both called out the openRxiv & Curvenote Reader project as an innovation in scientific publishing that they’d seen at the conference and were really excited about.

:::{figure #fig-ginny} ./images/force11-2026-ginny.png
Ginny Barbour’s closing keynote mentioned our openRxiv work specifically as something she was excited about.
:::

Ginny’s keynote captured this really well. It was about finally fixing scientific publishing, and it specifically mentioned [the openRxiv Curvenote partnership.](https://reader.openrxivlabs.org)

That meant a lot to me, because I know people of Ginny and Ian’s caliber have seen (and driven!) a lot of efforts to change publishing, so getting a callout from them is pretty motivating and affirming that we’re doing something special.

## The response to my own talk on Curvenote Reader was also surprising.

During the Q & A after [my own talk](https://youtu.be/jbnJAkj01Jc), I got the impression that even skeptical people were won over — maybe by the scale of our openRxiv Reader project, because it means we’re actually changing things — not just introducing some one-off prototypes. We have translated and deployed over 500k article versions with openRxiv to date.

People came up to me and asked specifically about converting their preprint to a Curvenote Reader upgraded preprint. To me, that says our article design is getting good enough to start attracting serious researchers. _And we’re still improving it._

Overall, a super inspiring event and I’m looking forward to the next one!
